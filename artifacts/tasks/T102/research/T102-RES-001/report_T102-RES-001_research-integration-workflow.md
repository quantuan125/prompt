# RESEARCH REPORT: T102 Research Integration & Workflow Governance

## I. Executive Summary

**Initiative T102** requires a cohesive approach to managing research within project initiation and governance workflows. This report addresses four interrelated goals: (A) how to integrate research findings into Project Initiation Documents (PIDs like the SPS) optimally, (B) how to formalize the research workflow with standard briefs, reports, and checkpoints, (C) how to govern the quality and risks of Large Language Model (LLM)-generated research, and (D) whether these improvements warrant a dedicated Epic or can be contained within existing scope. We find that **industry best practices strongly favor a structured yet flexible approach**: substantive research analysis should be **summarized in main governance documents with traceable references**, while detailed data is kept in appendices or external repositories. This balances stakeholder readability with document maintainability. To formalize the workflow, we recommend **standard templates for Research Briefs and Research Reports**, aligned with frameworks like ISO 29148 and BABOK v3 to ensure clarity, completeness, and traceability of requirements and their sources. Clear stage gates should validate research quality before findings inform project artifacts, echoing stage‐gate and PMI practices of using defined exit criteria before proceeding.

On LLM governance, the report highlights **critical risks** (hallucinations, traceability gaps, bias) and proposes **mitigation mechanisms**. These include mandatory source citation and verification for AI-generated content, human oversight as a "gatekeeper" for accuracy, maintaining an audit trail of AI usage, and tiered decision policies dictating when human expert research is required. Finally, the analysis concludes that **research workflow governance should be implemented via a focused Epic** (with its own features for templates, guidelines, and quality controls) due to its cross-cutting impact and complexity, while allowing interim improvements within T102A. A phased implementation plan is provided, defining new governance decision records (GDRs), integration guidelines, updated checklists, and clear ownership to ensure a smooth rollout of these enhancements.

## II. Deliverable A - Research Integration Pattern Report

### Placement of Research Content in PID (SPS) Documents

Integrating research findings into a PID-like SPS document requires balancing **comprehensiveness** with **clarity**. Industry standards indicate that core documents should remain concise and stakeholder-friendly, with detailed analyses available but not cluttering the main narrative. In practice, this translates to a tiered placement strategy:

- **Inline Summaries (Main Body):** Include a brief synopsis of key research findings directly in the SPS when they are crucial to understanding the project's context or decisions. For example, if a market analysis or feasibility study fundamentally shapes the project's scope or business case, a distilled summary (a few sentences or a short paragraph) belongs in the relevant section (e.g. assumptions, business context) of the PID. PRINCE2 guidelines reinforce that the PID should bundle information acquired during initiation, serving as a _reference point_ for the project. However, PRINCE2 and PMI also emphasize that PIDs/Charters remain high-level; detailed evidence and data are typically maintained in supporting documents or annexes. Thus, inline content should cover only the **essential insights** - the "so what" of the research - tailored for decision-makers.
- **Appendix Sections:** Lengthier research outputs (e.g. multi-page analyses, extensive datasets) should be relocated to appendices within the SPS or attached as annexes. An **appendix** allows the document to include full research reports or data tables without breaking the flow for the primary audience. Best practice in documentation (including academic writing) holds that appendices provide additional information that is helpful but not critical to the main narrative. For a PID, an appendix can house, for instance, a competitor analysis report or technical study results that stakeholders may reference as needed. This approach keeps the SPS lean and focused, while satisfying traceability (the evidence is available on record). It is advisable to clearly label and reference the appendix from the main text (e.g., "see Appendix B for detailed research findings") so that readers know more detail is available if required.
- **External Research Register or Repository:** For very **extensive or numerous research artifacts**, consider managing them outside the main SPS document altogether - in a dedicated research register, knowledge base, or repository - and referencing them in the SPS. Large programs often maintain separate repositories for supporting materials (e.g. a SharePoint library or Confluence space for project research) rather than embedding everything in the charter. PRINCE2's concept of _separate management products_ and PMI's separation of the project charter from subsidiary plans align with this approach. An external register would list each research item (with a unique ID, title, date, owner, and storage location) and a short abstract of findings. The SPS can then include an **indexed reference** (e.g., a footnote or a numbered reference) to that register entry. This method is scalable - whether there are 5 or 50 research documents, the SPS remains a manageable size, and the register provides structured traceability. It also supports parallel updates: researchers can update their documents in the repository without requiring major edits to the SPS (aside from potentially updating the summary or references). **Threshold criteria** for moving content external: if a research section would span more than e.g. ~300-500 words of text or if more than ~3-4 distinct research summaries would be needed in-line, it is a strong signal to externalize the bulk of that content. This threshold aligns with maintaining an SPS of reasonable length (most PIDs are in the tens of pages, not hundreds). In our case, the current pattern of embedding ~200-word "NOTE" summaries for each commissioned research is manageable for a few instances, but scaling to 5-10 such research tasks means 1,000+ words of notes cluttering the SPS - which crosses the threshold where alternative placement is warranted.

**Decision Criteria for Placement:** We propose a simple decision framework to decide **inline vs appendix vs external** for any given research output: - _Relevance:_ If the finding directly influences a section of the SPS (e.g., a key assumption, risk, or requirement) and must be immediately visible to understand the document, include a concise summary inline. If it provides background or justification that stakeholders might only occasionally need (e.g., detailed cost-benefit calculations), an appendix or external link is preferable. - _Length and Complexity:_ Use inline for very brief insights (a few sentences). Use an appendix for medium-length content (e.g. 1-3 pages of charts, tables, or text) that would overwhelm the section. Use external storage for anything beyond that scope or collections of many documents. - _Audience Needs:_ For stakeholder transparency and comfort, non-technical stakeholders often prefer the main document not to constantly send them elsewhere. Thus, critical high-level findings should be present in summary form in the SPS (to satisfy what PRINCE2 calls the Project Board's need for a one-stop view). However, technical teams or auditors who need details are comfortable retrieving an appendix or external doc. If an external reviewer or regulator will later audit decisions, having the full research reports available in the project files (but not necessarily in the main PID) is important for **auditability**. - _Update Frequency:_ Research that may be updated frequently (for example, an evolving market study) can be kept external to avoid frequent PID revisions - the SPS would reference "according to the Market Analysis Document (Living Doc ID#R3)…" and that external doc can be updated independently. Conversely, if the research is static and final, an appendix inclusion is fine. - _Risk and Criticality:_ If a particular research finding is controversial or high-risk (e.g., results of an AI ethics analysis influencing project go/no-go), putting at least the summary in the main document ensures visibility and formal sign-off. Routine or low-impact findings can safely reside in referenced materials.

By applying these criteria, we ensure **important insights aren't buried, and details aren't distracting**. This is consistent with guidance from standards: PMI's PMBOK notes that a charter includes high-level info and references the supporting details (like business case or feasibility study) rather than containing them wholly. Similarly, BABOK v3 advises that information elicited (like through document analysis) should be recorded in work products only at a level of detail appropriate for the audience and purpose - essentially, don't overload primary documents with minutiae better suited for appendices or separate files.

### Research Registers and Traceability Techniques

To manage scale and maintain traceability, implementing a **Research Artifact Register** is recommended once the volume of research outputs grows beyond a handful. This would be a controlled list (or table) of all research activities and outputs related to the initiative, functioning similar to a requirements traceability matrix or a risk register. Key fields in a research register could include: Research ID, Title/Topic, Brief Description of Findings, Author/Owner, Date, Related SPS Section or decision, and Location/Link to full document. For example:

| Research ID | Topic | Summary (one line) | Linked To | Location (Doc Ref) |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| R-001 | Feasibility Study - AI Module | Confirmed technical viability of X with 3rd-party API, showing 20% cost reduction potential. | Assumption A5, Risk R12 | Appendix A / File Share "Feasibility_AIModule.pdf" |
| R-002 | Market Analysis - EMEA Region | Identified top 5 user needs; product scope aligns except need for mobile app (not in original scope). | SPS Section III.B (Needs) | Research DB #23 (Confluence page) |
| R-003 | LLM Benchmarking (Tech) | Compared GPT-4 vs LLaMA on domain Q&A; GPT-4 had 15% hallucination rate, requiring verification step. | Concept Phase Plan, GDR-007 | External (GitHub repo "LLM_eval.md") |

Such a register can be maintained as an **appendix table** in the SPS for quick reference (if the list is short and static), or as a separate living document referenced from SPS if it will be frequently updated. The **industry precedent** for this is seen in large projects where _assumptions logs_, _issues registers_, and _lessons learned logs_ are kept separate but referenced in the PID/Charter. A research register is analogous - it provides one-stop traceability for all research contributions.

**Traceability Pattern:** Each research finding referenced in the SPS should explicitly tie to the source document or entry. This can be done via **markdown reference links or IDs** in the text (as we do with citations in this report). For example, an SPS statement derived from a research could end with a parenthetical like _(Ref: Research R-002)_ that corresponds to an entry in the research register or a footnote with a hyperlink to the full report. ISO 29148 (requirements engineering standard) underscores the importance of recording the **source** of each requirement. In our context, the "source" of a stated project need or design decision might be a research study. By treating research outputs as first-class traceable sources, we satisfy end-to-end traceability - every significant claim in the SPS can be traced to evidence. This practice also aligns with ISO 42010's mandate that **architecture decisions and their rationale be documented and linked to concerns and requirements**[**\[16\]**]. If a research provides rationale for a decision (say, adopting Technology X), then documenting that linkage (decision ↔ research reference) is crucial for future audits and for architecture governance.

**Verbosity & Structure:** To avoid bloat when referencing research in-line, use a concise citation style. The current SPS uses "NOTE" sections which embed a ~200-word summary. Going forward, we can reduce redundancy by not re-narrating the entire research, but summarizing _just the actionable insight_, and then referencing the source. For instance: _"The client base in EU is expected to grow 25% annually (per Market Study R-005), supporting the aggressive expansion assumption."_ - This sentence gives the takeaway and a pointer. Contrast this with copying a whole paragraph of the market study - unnecessary and hard to maintain if the study gets updated. Additionally, if one research finding is relevant to multiple parts of the SPS (initiative-level and an epic-level consideration), avoid duplicating the text; instead, reference the same research ID in both places. Our internal analysis flagged duplication risk when one research note was relevant to both Section III.B.7 (Initiative Notes) and an Epic's notes. The solution is to write the full note once (probably at the higher scope) and elsewhere just refer to it ("see Note 3 in III.B.7 for related research"). This ensures **single source of truth** for each finding.

**Maintaining Readability:** One concern with externalizing content is that it could force readers to chase references, potentially violating the ease-of-reading quality goal (in T102, **T102-QG-001 mandates client readability**). To address this, any critical research that a non-technical stakeholder _must_ understand should still be present in summary form as discussed. Less critical references can be slightly deemphasized (e.g., footnotes). For example, a footnote might contain the full reference to an external doc without cluttering the main text. This approach is common in policy briefs and whitepapers: the main text states conclusions, and footnotes provide the supporting source for those who wish to verify details. The use of hyperlinked digital PIDs also mitigates this - clicking a reference can open the detailed report, making it fairly seamless.

In summary, **best practices for research integration in PIDs** are: - Keep the PID's main content focused and concise, embedding only essential research insights. - Offload extensive data and reports to appendices or external documents, with clear referencing. - Implement a structured index (register) of research artifacts for traceability and governance. - Ensure each requirement or decision in the PID can trace back to a credible source (research or otherwise), satisfying standards of traceability (as per ISO and INCOSE guidelines). - Control the volume of inline research text by using summary+reference patterns, thereby managing scale as the number of research items grows.

Adopting these patterns will prevent the **"document bloat"** and maintenance overhead observed in the current state, while maintaining stakeholder trust that all statements are evidence-backed (even if the evidence is in an annex). Real-world case studies show that organizations that attempted to embed full analyses in charters eventually faced versioning and size issues; the more sustainable approach was to treat the charter as a gateway with pointers to an **evidence library**. Our recommendations essentially formalize that library concept for T102.

## III. Deliverable B - Research Workflow Formalization Report

### Standard Structures for Research Briefs and Reports

A formal **research workflow** within the consultancy process should be underpinned by standardized document templates and procedures. Currently, the initiative has been using ad-hoc research briefs (e.g., research_sps-workflow-optimization_T102A_v1.0.0_i1.md) and lengthy AI-generated reports, but without a defined structure or template. Industry standards and methodologies suggest consistent formats to ensure completeness and quality. We recommend establishing two key artifact templates: a **Research Brief (commissioning document)** and a **Research Report (deliverable document)**, each with a clear structure.

**Research Brief Template:** This is the document created _before_ research execution (essentially the "terms of reference" for the research). It ensures that the purpose, scope, and questions are well-defined. Drawing from standards like **BABOK v3** (which emphasizes well-formed elicitation context) and organizational practices: - The brief should start with an **Executive Summary** stating _why_ the research is being commissioned and what decision or issue it will inform. For example: _"This research brief commissions an investigation into X to address issue Y that is blocking project Z"_. In T102, the briefs already begin with context (e.g., noting it's to resolve T102-ISSUE-005), which is good practice. - **Research Objectives/Questions:** Enumerate the primary research questions the effort should answer. These should be specific and framed in an industry context if possible (e.g., "What are industry-standard criteria for ...?"). - **Scope and Constraints:** Clarify what is in and out of scope. This might include defining the depth of research (literature review vs. quick survey), any time constraints, and resources available. - **Methodology Approach:** Outline how the research will be conducted (sources to be consulted, frameworks to use). For instance, indicate that both internal documents and external standards (PRINCE2, PMBOK, ISO, etc.) will be reviewed, and that peer-reviewed literature will be included for certain questions. Citing ISO 29148 or BABOK here can ensure alignment with recognized analysis processes (ISO 29148 describes iterative requirements analysis, which can be mirrored in iterative research analysis). - **Deliverables:** List the expected outputs (which correspond to sections A-E in this case). This sets expectations for format (e.g., a report with specific sections, any tables or models). - **Validation and Review Plan:** It's important to state how the research output will be validated or reviewed. E.g., "LLM_Researcher will produce a draft report to be reviewed by \[Client/Consultant\] for accuracy and completeness before acceptance." This acts as a built-in quality gate (see **Validation Gates** below). - **Timeline and Sign-off:** State the expected turnaround (e.g., "research to be completed within 2 days") and include a sign-off section where the commissioning authority (client or lead consultant) approves the brief. The current briefs have a "Client Approval" checklist and signature line, which aligns with best practice: no research should proceed without stakeholder buy-in on its necessity and scope.

This template ensures every research commission is **well-structured from the start**, preventing scope creep and focusing the researcher on relevant questions. It mirrors what a **Project Product Description** or **feasibility study request** might contain in PRINCE2 terms (PRINCE2 encourages clearly defining what an external study should achieve). It also resonates with **BABOK's approach to planning business analysis**: identify what techniques (research, document analysis, etc.) will be used and how results will be captured.

**Research Report Template:** This is the output from the research execution (what the researcher delivers back). To align with professional standards and **ISO documentation norms**, the report should be structured for clarity and verification: - **Executive Summary:** A concise summary of findings and recommendations (if any) - likely 1-2 paragraphs. Many stakeholders will read only this section, so it should standalone in conveying the key answers and suggested actions. - **Introduction:** Background and context, reiterating the questions being answered and why they matter. It can reference the commissioning brief and any context (like "this research was prompted by Issue X affecting the SPS process"). This sets the stage and ensures traceability (tying back to the brief's objectives). - **Methodology:** Describe how the research was conducted. Listing sources consulted (internal files, specific standards, publications) gives transparency. For example, mention that PRINCE2 manual, PMI's PMBOK Guide, BABOK v3, ISO standards, and academic papers were analyzed. This section enhances credibility (readers see breadth of research) and allows an auditor to replicate or verify the work by checking those references. - **Findings/Discussion:** This will be the bulk, organized ideally by the research questions or thematic areas. For instance, in our case, subsections for: A. Integration Patterns, B. Workflow Formalization, C. LLM Governance, D. Epic Scope. Under each, present synthesized findings from external sources and internal analysis, complete with citations. A best practice from consulting reports is to first present relevant _industry best practices or standards_ ("what does the literature say?"), then discuss the _implications or gaps_ for our specific situation. The example report for T102C (Concept roadmap) we have shows this style: it enumerated standard guidance (PRINCE2, PMI, ISO) then deduced implications. We should emulate that: e.g., list what PRINCE2/PMBOK/SAFe say about research integration or stage gates, then what that implies for us. - **Conclusions/Recommendations:** Clearly answer the primary questions and give actionable recommendations. If multiple options were considered, this is where we state the chosen path and why (just as the T102C example report had a "Recommendation" section choosing an option and justifying it). For our report, this might include recommending Option A/B/C for the epic scope, and summarizing the needed governance changes. - **References:** A section listing all sources cited (standards, articles, etc.). In formal reports, using a consistent citation style (IEEE, APA, etc.) is important. Given our environment, we might use the footnote/citation format as demonstrated. Ensuring that every external claim is cited addresses traceability and credibility. For instance, if we say "PMI recommends a summary milestone schedule in charters," we cite PMBOK or a PMI source. If we say "LLMs have a known hallucination rate ~20-30% in studies," we cite a study or survey.

By institutionalizing these templates, we layer in quality. **ISO/IEC/IEEE 29148** emphasizes the importance of templates for requirement specs to ensure all necessary sections are covered. Analogously, our research templates ensure no critical aspect (like methodology or validation) is omitted. They also make it easier to compare and reuse research outputs since each report will have the same structure (facilitating a "checklist review" by leads - see next point).

### Validation Gates and Artifact Lifecycle in the Workflow

Formalizing the research workflow isn't just about documents, but also **process checkpoints (quality gates)** and integration into the overall project lifecycle (SPS → Request → Concept). We propose introducing two gated quality control points: 1. **Gate 1: Research Brief Approval** - Before research starts (after the brief is written). The brief must be reviewed and signed-off by the decision owner (the client or initiative lead). This is effectively what we see at the end of current briefs - a checklist for the client to ensure the scope is comprehensive and aligned with needs, and that success criteria are clear. Only an approved brief triggers the research commission to proceed. This gate prevents wasted effort on poorly defined or unnecessary research. It mirrors the idea of a Phase Gate in project management where a feasibility study or analysis phase must be approved to advance. It also ensures accountability; the requestor can't later say the research missed the mark if they approved the plan. In ISO terms, this is akin to a verification of inputs: making sure the question is right before seeking the answer.

- **Gate 2: Research Report Quality Check** - After the draft report is produced by the researcher (LLM or human). Before its findings are integrated into any SPS/Request/Concept artifact, the report should undergo a validation review. This could involve the main consultant or a subject matter expert reviewing: **(a)** factual accuracy (are the cited standards correctly interpreted?), **(b)** completeness (all questions answered?), and **(c)** clarity (is it understandable to the intended audience?). In our LLM context, this step is critical to catch AI-specific issues (hallucinations, misquotes) before they propagate. Established practice in consulting is peer review of any research deliverable especially if junior analysts or automated tools produced it. Concretely, this gate might be implemented as a checklist similar to: "All statements are backed by sources; No contradictions to known organizational policies; Key insights are clearly identified; Recommendations are actionable; Document is free of hallucinated content (all citations verify correct)." Only after passing this check (and any rework done) is the research "accepted."

These validation gates enforce **quality control and traceability** at two critical junctures. They align with **BABOK's Verification and Validation tasks** for business analysis information - ensuring the information is sufficient and correct before use. They also answer the question: how do we trust an AI-generated report? The answer is by not skipping the human-in-the-loop verification before acceptance (more on LLM governance in Section C).

Between these gates, the workflow includes the **research execution phase** (which for us may involve LLM_Researcher agent doing the heavy lifting). During execution, intermediate steps can be taken to ensure quality, such as: if it's a particularly complex topic, maybe a quick check-in (midpoint review) can be done to ensure the research is on the right track. Agile practices can be borrowed here (e.g., time-box the research and do a brief stand-up with the team on findings to date).

### Integrating Research into SPS → Request → Concept Phases

The consultancy workflow for T102 spans three artifact stages: SPS (the problem definition), Request (detailed requirements analysis), and Concept (solution design). We need to articulate **where and when research activities should occur in this flow and how their outputs are utilized**. In essence, we are layering an orthogonal "research stream" across these phases:

- **During SPS Phase (Initiation/Discovery):** This is where exploratory research is most needed. The SPS artifact represents an initial, often ambiguous problem exploration. Common research needs in this phase include understanding business context (market or user research), identifying relevant regulations or standards that impose constraints, and learning from prior lessons (lessons learned or analogous projects). For example, if the client's idea is cutting-edge, SPS phase might commission background research on industry best practices or feasibility. PRINCE2 and PMBOK both acknowledge such analysis in initiation: the PID often builds on a _feasibility study_ or _business case research_ done prior or in parallel. Thus, our framework should expect **research briefs to be triggered in SPS** for any unknowns that the consultant cannot answer from experience. The outputs (reports) from SPS-stage research feed directly into the SPS document (or its appendices) before it is finalized. They might also produce governance decisions (GDRs) if, say, a research finding leads to a new approach or constraint (e.g., a study might result in a governance rule "We must include data encryption - confirmed required by regulation" leading to a requirement and a GDR noting regulatory compliance). Essentially, in SPS we formalize the _discovery research_ needed to clarify the problem.
- **During Request Phase (Requirements Elaboration):** Once we move to the Request artifact, the project is more defined, but research can still play a role. Here it is more targeted: e.g., researching specific solution requirements, technology options for certain features, or doing cost analysis research for different approaches. The Request document (similar to a Software Requirements Specification or Business Requirements Document) might commission research to validate requirements or to gather benchmark data (like "what response time do users expect? Let's research UX studies."). In agile terms, some of these are like **"spikes"** (time-boxed research/prototyping tasks) to answer questions about requirements. Our workflow should integrate research by allowing the **Request procedural guidelines** to call for research tasks when needed. For instance, if a requirement is identified as high-risk or unclear, the process could be: flag it and initiate a research brief to investigate (instead of guessing). The outputs at this stage would feed into refining the requirements or adding details (and possibly creating Concept phase input). **Traceability:** any research done in the Request stage should trace to the specific requirement or design decision it supports, maintaining that line of sight for auditors (which requirement came from which evidence).
- **During Concept Phase (Solution Design & Conceptual Architecture):** At this stage, research typically revolves around solution approaches - evaluating alternatives, technologies, and architectural patterns. In T102's context, Concept is an artifact that presumably outlines potential solution concepts or high-level designs (akin to an architectural brief or options analysis). Research in this phase might include proofs-of-concept, literature review on technologies (e.g., "Is blockchain viable for this use case? See research on blockchain scalability."), or consulting known architectural standards. If the consultant (or an LLM agent) is formulating a concept, they might commission targeted research on each candidate solution's pros/cons or reference architecture. **Architecture frameworks** like TOGAF call for an "Architecture Background" or reference models which often require research to compile. Our formal workflow should treat these as well: concept phase research briefs can be issued for deep dives into specific questions (like "what does literature say about microservices vs monolith for X domain?"). The results will directly inform the Concept artifact's content (and perhaps feed into Architecture Decision Records, ADRs, if used). Additionally, by Concept phase, the **LLM context window limitation** starts to bite - the SPS and Request are big by now, and an LLM might not hold everything. Formal research outputs at this phase can serve to **offload context**: rather than try to have the LLM recall all details, it can incorporate summarized research that has been validated. In this way, formal research artifacts also become a strategy for managing AI context (we provide the LLM with distilled, relevant information rather than raw massive docs).
- **Post-Concept / Pre-Implementation:** Although not explicitly asked, note that even during design or implementation (epic execution), research can occur (e.g., a developer might research a specific algorithm). Those would be beyond consultancy phase (handed to LLM_Developer perhaps), but the governance we set now can be a model for later phases too. T102D (Design) might likewise benefit from a research workflow for technical decisions.

**Workflow Integration Protocol:** We recommend updating the **procedural guidelines** (SPSPG, Request PG, Concept PG) to incorporate triggers for research. Concretely: - In the **SPS Procedural Guideline (SPSPG)**: Add a step after initial problem statement drafting: _"Identify knowledge gaps or open issues that require research. For each, initiate a Research Brief (template) and get approval."_ Also, instruct that by the end of SPS development, all research notes must either be closed or handed off (traceably) to next phases. - In the **Request Guideline (RPG)**: When fleshing out features and requirements, include a task: _"For any requirement with uncertainty (e.g., marked TBD or high risk), consider commissioning research or a prototype spike."_ Ensure that results of that research update the requirements and are captured (perhaps as part of the "Analysis" subsection of a requirement or in an appended study). - In the **Concept Guideline (CPG)**: Add: _"During solution option analysis, for each option, gather evidence or research backing its viability._ If multiple solution patterns exist, research each (cost, precedent, etc.)." The guideline should point to using the Research Brief/Report process for formal investigations as needed. - **Handoff points:** The transitions between phases should explicitly include research outputs. For example, at SPS → Request handoff, include in the acceptance criteria that all critical open issues have either been researched or are explicitly flagged for research in the Request phase (with owner assigned). Similarly, Request → Concept handoff should check that any research necessary for design (e.g., technical feasibility studies) is planned or done. This idea matches industry stage gate criteria, where moving to the next phase requires no significant unknowns without a mitigation plan.

**Templates and Checklists:** Each artifact's **Validation Checklist** (often present in templates) should integrate research-related checks. For instance, the SPS template's checklist can ask: "Have all research findings been properly summarized and referenced (not too verbose, not missing)?" and "Is each project assumption or major decision supported by evidence or marked as a hypothesis to research?" The Request checklist might include: "Were regulatory/standards requirements validated by research?" and "Are outstanding questions documented with an action (research or prototyping)?". The Concept checklist can include: "Alternatives analysis includes references to research or benchmarks used" and a check that any claims (like "Solution A is more scalable") are backed by data or citation. By structuring these into checklists, we enforce that the integration is happening - much like a quality audit.

Alignment with **ISO 29148**: This standard says requirements need rationale and source; our approach ensures that by formalizing the way sources (research) are obtained and attached to requirements. **BABOK v3** provides techniques for validation like _Reviews_ and _Document Analysis_; our gates incorporate review, and our integrated workflow ensures any document analysis (research) is systematically fed into the process rather than ad-hoc. We are effectively layering an **evidence-based requirements engineering** approach, where decisions are based on researched facts.

Finally, we must ensure the entire approach is documented in the **SPS Procedural Guideline (SPSPG)** as a process. For example, SPSPG Section on "Notes & Parking Lot" should state that research notes follow the new pattern (short summary + reference ID). The guideline for "Governance updates" should mention capturing outcomes as GDRs if needed (e.g., if a research yields a governance rule). By formalizing in the procedure, future consultants and LLM agents will know how to execute it step by step.

In summary, formalizing the research workflow introduces **template consistency, defined roles and checkpoints, and integration with phase deliverables**. This reduces the current ad-hoc nature (where research notes were appended somewhat informally) and instead treats research as a first-class part of the project lifecycle - with its own mini life-cycle (plan → do → check → integrate). The result will be higher-quality governance documents that can stand up to scrutiny (internal or external) because every key piece of information is validated and traceable.

## IV. Deliverable C - LLM Research Governance Framework

The use of Large Language Model (LLM) agents (such as _ChatGPT Deep Research_) to generate research content introduces unique **risks and challenges** that must be governed to maintain quality and trust. We identify the critical risks associated with LLM-generated research, assess their severity, and propose governance mechanisms and mitigation patterns for each. We organize these findings by risk category, roughly in order of severity (highest impact risks first). Our analysis is informed by emerging academic literature on AI reliability as well as industry lessons from real-world AI adoption cases.

### Risk 1: **Hallucinations and Factual Errors**

**Description:** _Hallucination_ in LLMs refers to the model producing outputs that are factually incorrect or entirely fabricated, despite often sounding plausible. In the context of research, this could mean the LLM cites non-existent studies, provides incorrect data points, or draws false conclusions. This is a **critical risk** because it directly undermines the integrity of the research deliverable. If not caught, hallucinations could lead to wrong project decisions (e.g., relying on a fake regulation or misinterpreted best practice). Academic surveys indicate hallucination is a prevalent issue: recent studies show that even state-of-the-art models hallucinate facts with alarming frequency, sometimes **20-30% of the time or more** in complex domains. For instance, an analysis of AI legal research tools found systems hallucinating in ~17-33% of answers, and one tool produced falsehoods in the majority of its outputs. These hallucinations can be subtle - e.g., an LLM might cite a real standard but misquote its content or attribute it to the wrong context. The impact is high: decisions based on such misinformation can fail or cause compliance issues. Real-world case: an attorney using ChatGPT included six fake case citations in a brief; the error was only caught when the opposing side and judge found the cases didn't exist, leading to sanctions. This underscores that _human trust in AI output without verification can have serious consequences_.

**Governance & Mitigations:** We must implement robust **quality control measures** specifically targeting hallucinations: - **Source Verification Protocol:** Any factual claim or reference produced by the LLM must be verified against a reliable source _before_ it is accepted into our documents. This could mean the LLM is prompted to provide sources (as we do with this Citations style), and then a human (or an automated secondary check) confirms each source actually supports the claim. For example, if ChatGPT says "According to ISO 12207, X…", one must check ISO 12207 to ensure X is correctly quoted or summarized. If the LLM cannot provide a source, that information should be treated as suspect unless confirmed through independent research. - **Use of Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG):** One way to reduce hallucination is to supply the LLM with a knowledge base (for instance, upload the text of relevant ISO standards or internal docs so it can quote from them). This grounds the model in actual reference text rather than relying on its trained memory. For future development, integrating a company-specific knowledge retrieval (embedding relevant PDFs, etc.) could significantly mitigate hallucinations since the model would have the text to back up statements. In essence, make the LLM a smarter research assistant that always provides citations from a given library. - **Chain-of-Thought and Prompt Engineering:** Research shows certain prompting techniques (like asking the model to reason step-by-step or to double-check answers) can reduce hallucinations. We should adopt prompt patterns that encourage the model to evaluate its own answer. For instance, after an answer, ask the model "Is every statement above supported by known sources? If not, identify them." This can catch some issues. - **Human-in-the-Loop Review:** The Gate 2 validation we described in Section B is critical here. A knowledgeable human (or at least someone with the patience to fact-check) must review the LLM's output. If internal expertise is lacking on some detail, one mitigation is to have a second LLM instance cross-check (but ultimately a human should make the call). Another idea is to engage an **AI "red team"** approach: prompt a separate LLM (or same with a different prompt) to find flaws or counterarguments to the first LLM's report. This technique can sometimes surface hallucinations (the second model might say "I cannot find source for claim Y"). - **Approved Sources List:** Governance can dictate that LLM research should rely on certain authoritative sources. For instance, prefer direct quotes from official standards, academic papers, or well-known industry publications. Discourage or disallow content that the model "knows" but cannot attribute. This might mean instructing: "If a claim cannot be sourced, it must be omitted or explicitly flagged as analyst assumption." - **Testing & Metrics:** Establish metrics for LLM output factual accuracy. For example, during trial runs, measure how many factual errors are caught per report. If our process sees, say, on average 2 hallucinations per 10,000 tokens and they're all caught, we're doing well. If many slip by, we need to tighten controls. As part of governance, regular audits of past research could be done to ensure no undetected hallucinations lurk.

**Residual Risk:** With these measures, hallucination risk can be greatly reduced but not entirely eliminated. The **severity** (impact) remains high (any one severe hallucination could misdirect a project), but the **likelihood** can be reduced from probable to unlikely with vigilant verification. We should treat any AI-written content as _provisional_ until verified - that principle should be codified in our governance policy (e.g., a GDR that states "LLM-generated research findings are not considered authoritative until verified by a human or trusted source," effectively making unverified AI content inadmissible as project evidence).

### Risk 2: **Lack of Traceability & Opaqueness**

**Description:** Traditional research documentation allows traceability - one can see references, follow footnotes, and audit how conclusions were drawn. LLM-generated content, if not properly handled, can become a "black box." The model might give an answer without clear origin for the information, making it hard to trace back to raw data or source documents. This is partly related to hallucinations, but even when the info is correct, we need to maintain **audit trails**. For example, if our SPS is challenged in a review ("Why did we assume 25% growth?"), and the answer is "the AI said so, citing some source we didn't keep," that's unacceptable. **Auditing and compliance** standards (like those discussed in AI governance frameworks and the emerging EU AI Act) demand transparency and the ability to reconstruct how AI was used in decision-making. Additionally, if our LLM interactions aren't logged, we lose institutional knowledge and can't review past rationales.

**Governance & Mitigations:** - **Force Citations and Logging:** Our current practice of requiring the LLM to provide citations for its statements should be mandatory. The system (our research process) should never accept a naked assertion from the AI - always ask for "according to \[source\]" format or something equivalent. Then, critically, we must **log the entire interaction** with the LLM (questions asked, answers given, with citations). These logs should be stored as part of project records (perhaps not in the main SPS, but in an accessible archive). This aligns with Deloitte's guidance that a reliable AI process needs a thorough audit trail of inputs and outputs. If using tools, ensure that conversation history is saved and linkable. - **Metadata in Documents:** Each research report produced by LLM should include a methodology section (as we outlined) listing sources. That inherently provides traceability (someone reading the report can follow those references). We should consider appending the raw chat Q&A as an annex for full transparency (maybe not in the polished report sent to client, but in internal documentation). - **Precedence Rules:** Implement a governance rule that _no governance decision or requirement is to be accepted based solely on LLM output without reference._ If an LLM provides an insight that we couldn't find a source for, we either reject it or treat it as a hypothesis to be validated by a human expert or further research. In other words, **LLM is a research aid, not an oracle**. This principle needs to be ingrained to avoid lazy acceptance of unsourced info. - **Traceability Matrix:** Expand the concept of traceability to explicitly map AI contributions. For example, maintain a mapping of each GDR or important conclusion to either a human source or an AI source (with log references). **ISO 24765** (glossary) and **INCOSE** emphasize that traceability is key to system quality; in AI use, we might have to trace not just requirements to sources, but AI outputs to their validation. An internal audit might ask: "We see you used an AI to write this report. Show us that everything the AI stated is traceable to an authoritative source or a human decision." We should be able to produce that. - **Tooling & Automation:** If possible, integrate tools that automatically attach sources in a bibliography when the AI produces content. Some AI research tools (as mentioned in academic literature) promise "100% hallucination-free linked citations" - while that is an optimistic claim, the idea is to leverage any tech that can increase linkages. For instance, using plugins or retrieval that provide citations from a known set (like having the AI retrieve from a vector database of known texts ensures each sentence is grounded). - **Transparency to Stakeholders:** We must also be transparent to the ultimate project stakeholders that LLM assistance was used. While not everyone needs the detail, a note in the methodology or footnote like "This section was generated with the aid of OpenAI's ChatGPT and verified by the team" could be included for full disclosure. This is in line with emerging AI ethics guidelines which call for transparency about AI involvement. It preempts trust issues; if later a minor error is found, stakeholders won't be blindsided that AI was involved.

**Residual considerations:** The severity of losing traceability is high in the sense of governance compliance (especially if ever under audit or if a decision fails and one asks "how did this get approved?"). By enforcing strong logging and citation practices, we turn LLM outputs into traceable artifacts. The overhead is manageable (it's mostly about discipline and possibly some supporting tech). The organization should treat the AI like a junior analyst whose work must be documented and reviewed - a notion recommended by many AI governance experts. This mindset will maintain the chain of accountability.

### Risk 3: **Bias and Incompleteness**

**Description:** LLMs can reflect biases present in their training data, potentially skewing research results. For instance, ChatGPT might prioritize Western sources or well-known frameworks while missing others, leading to a one-sided view. If our LLM researcher summarizes industry best practices but its training data had little about, say, Chinese project management standards or niche agile practices, the report might be inadvertently biased or incomplete. There's also risk of the AI **omitting** important factors because it doesn't know about them or wasn't asked correctly - e.g., maybe it answers about PRINCE2 and PMI and SAFe (common in English data) but ignores ISO 21500 or others less in its corpus. The BABOK, for example, might not be well represented in the model's knowledge if not commonly discussed. This risk is moderate in impact (it might not introduce outright falsehood like hallucinations, but it can lead to suboptimal decisions if alternative viewpoints are missed). Bias could also manifest in style - e.g., the AI using overly optimistic language or not highlighting uncertainties, which could mislead decision makers about the confidence level of findings.

**Governance & Mitigations:** - **Diverse Data Sources:** Ensure the research prompt or process includes diverse perspectives. For example, explicitly ask the LLM for multiple frameworks ("Consider PRINCE2, PMI PMBOK, ISO standards, IIBA BABOK, etc." as we did). If we only ask generally, it might default to whichever it "remembers" best. By structuring the research questions to enumerate specific bodies of knowledge, we guide the AI to cover them. Our research briefs can list the target reference models (as seen in the brief listing PRINCE2, PMBOK, SAFe, ISO, BABOK, etc. as primary standards to consult). This helps mitigate omission of any major source. - **Bias Awareness Checks:** Introduce a step where either the LLM or the human reviewer analyzes the output for potential bias or blind spots. For instance, after getting the answer, we could ask the LLM, "Are there any perspectives or standards that might have different views not covered here?" Sometimes the model might then mention something it left out (maybe a region-specific standard or an alternative approach) - effectively performing a consistency check. Also, encourage the human reviewer to consider diversity: if the output only cites North American standards, consider if there are European or other international sources (ISO we have, what about say IPMA or local gov guidelines?). - **Use of Multiple Models or Versions:** Different LLMs have different training data. We could, for crucial research, query another model (like an open model or one fine-tuned on different data) to see if it brings up points the first didn't. This is an emerging practice - akin to seeking a second opinion. It's perhaps not always practical, but for high-stakes content, it might be worth it. - **Human Domain Expertise:** For now, AI isn't a true expert - it patterns existing text. If we have access to human experts or stakeholders with domain experience, involve them in reviewing the content for completeness. For example, our client or a senior consultant might know of a relevant standard the AI missed. Governance could specify that research reports be circulated to a small group for feedback, including "Have we missed any approach you're aware of?" - a manual but effective way to catch incompleteness. - **Bias in Language and Tone:** We should also moderate the tone of AI output. Models like ChatGPT might overstate confidence. We can adjust prompts to ensure balanced phrasing (like instructing it to include pros and cons, mention any controversies, and use hedging where appropriate). The governance policy might require that AI-generated text is reviewed for any unintended persuasive bias or inconsistent style with our usual reports. (This is more of a style/ethics check but still important for stakeholder trust.)

**Mapping to risk severity:** Bias/incompleteness is perhaps lower severity than factual errors because its impact is more subtle (you might still make a correct decision, just not the optimal one). But in aggregate, if our research consistently misses certain viewpoints, it could systematically skew project decisions. With mitigations, we can keep this risk low. It's similar to having a single human researcher who might have blind spots - the solution is similar: review and diversify sources. There are known frameworks for AI fairness and bias evaluation (e.g., NIST AI Risk Management Framework addresses bias as a key function); we should follow the spirit of those: identify potential biases, document them, and mitigate by broadening data.

### Risk 4: **Over-reliance and Automation Bias**

**Description:** A less technical but very real risk is _psychological_: stakeholders or team members might overly trust the LLM's outputs because they come from an "AI" and are delivered quickly and eloquently. This is known as automation bias - the tendency to favor suggestions from an automated decision-maker. Conversely, there's also risk of _under_\-trust if stakeholders know AI was used and therefore doubt the content arbitrarily. Both extremes are problematic. If over-relied upon, team members might skip their own critical thinking ("the AI did the research, we're done"). If under-trusted, the value of the research is undermined and people might redo work from scratch, losing efficiency. We have to navigate this carefully, especially with clients or decision owners who may have varying levels of confidence in AI-derived content.

**Governance & Mitigations:** - **Clarity on AI's Role:** Formally document and communicate that the LLM is a tool that assists human consultants, not a replacement. Emphasize that final accountability lies with human experts. This can be in the methodology or in a governance policy that states how AI is to be used. For example, "LLM_Researcher outputs are treated as draft analysis to be verified - not as final truth." If stakeholders understand that process, they can have appropriate trust levels. - **Quality Assurance and Human Sign-off:** Ensure that every AI-generated report has a named human owner who signs off after review (essentially attesting, "I have reviewed this and believe it to be accurate and suitable"). This reinstates human accountability. In T102, the LLM_Consultant (main) could be required to sign off on the LLM_Researcher's output before it's delivered to the client or used in the SPS. Knowing that a human took responsibility can calibrate stakeholder trust better than "an AI wrote this." - **Training and Awareness:** Those involved (especially the LLM_Consultant using the tool) should be trained in cognitive biases. They should consciously cross-check things even if the AI seems confident. The governance framework could suggest techniques like intentionally double-checking one "fact" per section to keep one's skeptical edge, or adopting a "trust but verify" culture. - **If in Doubt, Seek Human Expert:** For any conclusions that just don't sit right or are very high-stakes (like legal or regulatory interpretations), governance should say: involve a human subject matter expert. For instance, if the AI research says "This project likely doesn't need GDPR compliance because X," we should probably have a privacy officer confirm that rather than taking the AI's word. Knowing when to escalate to a human expert is part of good judgment; we can encode some triggers (e.g., legal issues -> always have legal counsel review). - **Monitor Outcomes:** Over time, track if there are instances where AI-based research led to errors or near-misses. Use those as learning to adjust trust. For example, if one time an unchecked hallucination almost slipped, remind everyone why the process caught it and what would have happened if not. Keeping a record of "AI got this right/wrong" can help maintain a healthy skepticism or build confidence where deserved.

### Risk 5: **Security and Confidentiality**

_(The prompt didn't explicitly mention this, but it's worth noting in an LLM governance context.)_

**Description:** If using external AI services (like OpenAI's API), there's risk that sensitive project information could be exposed. The queries we send might contain details of the client's idea or internal data. Also, the AI might output information that is proprietary or should not be widely shared, but if we treat it like normal research, we might inadvertently distribute content inappropriately. Ensuring confidentiality and compliance (especially if data is regulated) is key.

**Governance & Mitigations:** - **Data Handling Policies:** Define what can and cannot be shared with the LLM. Perhaps avoid inputting any client-identifying info or specific financials unless the LLM environment is secured or on-prem. If using a cloud AI, use pseudonyms or abstractions in the prompt (we can transform specifics into general terms for the AI to reason with, then map back internally). - **Non-Disclosure and Terms:** Ensure the AI service provider is under adequate data protection terms (OpenAI now provides options for not storing data when using their API, etc.). The governance should state that the use of AI must comply with the company's data security policies (maybe requiring using approved instances or anonymizing content). - **Review AI Outputs for Sensitive Info:** The AI might regurgitate text from its training data that wasn't meant for our project context (unlikely in research mode, but e.g., it might produce a snippet from a source that is copyrighted - which can pose IP issues, or it might mention something about a competitor that is sensitive). We also recommend a **Risk Register entry** for "LLM usage in research" to ensure continuous monitoring. Each risk above can be an item with its mitigation actions tracked.

To prioritize: **Hallucination risk** and **traceability** are top priority (they have highest impact), followed by ensuring completeness and managing trust. In incorporating external research on AI governance, we align with the views of experts: e.g., the **Harvard CorpGov article** suggests audit committees oversee AI use, emphasizing internal controls and oversight for accuracy and compliance. Ultimately, by implementing these governance mechanisms, we transform the LLM from a potential loose cannon into a powerful assistant that operates within clearly defined guardrails. The **severity of LLM-related failures can be brought down to an acceptable level**, where using the AI speeds up research but the outcome quality is on par with (or even better than) fully manual research, thanks to stringent verification and comprehensive traceability. The framework will foster stakeholder confidence that "AI-generated" does _not_ mean "unreliable" in our processes - instead it will mean "efficiently produced, thoroughly vetted."

## V. Deliverable D - Epic Scope Recommendation

**Key Question:** Based on the complexity and breadth of the improvements outlined in A, B, and C, should the research workflow and governance enhancements be implemented as a separate epic (dedicated workstream) or integrated within the existing T102A epic (SPS Workflow) or elsewhere?

After careful evaluation, **we recommend treating "Research Workflow & Governance" as a distinct Epic** (let's call it T102E) rather than trying to bundle all changes into T102A. This recommendation is backed by both a complexity/impact analysis and industry analogs for managing cross-cutting concerns.

### Rationale for a Separate Epic (Option A)

- **Scope and Complexity:** The changes involve multiple facets: template creation (SPS research sections, new Research Brief/Report templates), procedural guideline updates across artifacts, new governance rules (GDRs), and possibly tooling/process changes for the LLM governance. This is a substantial body of work that spans beyond just the SPS document structure. It touches the Initiative level (common standards across SPS, Request, Concept) and likely will involve coordination with other epics (Request workflow T102B, Concept T102C, etc.). In agile terms, this work constitutes an **Enabler Epic** - it's an architectural/process enabler that benefits multiple value streams (the consultancy workflow overall). SAFe suggests encapsulating such significant enablement work into its own epic to ensure it gets proper visibility and resources. If we tried to force all these tasks under T102A, we risk overloading that epic's scope (which is already focused on SPS template/story content). Option C (making it a separate initiative entirely) might be overkill unless we foresee a whole program on AI governance; currently, it fits within the boundaries of T102's objectives (improving the consultancy layer), so an epic within T102 is sufficient.
- **Focus and Ownership:** A dedicated epic can have a focused owner (maybe the _LLM_Consultant - Research Lead_ or a designated process architect) and team (perhaps including the LLM_Researcher role and QA roles) to drive these changes. This avoids diluting the focus of the T102A team, which might otherwise prioritize deliverables of the SPS template itself over the meta-process improvements. With a separate epic, we ensure that research workflow formalization and AI governance don't fall through the cracks or keep getting deferred because the SPS template "immediate" tasks take precedence. Essentially, it creates a **parallel track** that acknowledges "this work is as important as the product features."
- **Impact on Multiple Artifacts:** The improvements will produce outputs that need integration into **SPS (T102A)**, **Request (T102B)**, **Concept (T102C)**, and potentially beyond (future initiatives using LLM research). By structuring it as an epic, it can produce clearly versioned deliverables: e.g., _RBST - Research Brief Structural Template_, _RRST - Research Report Structural Template_, _RPG updates - Research Process Guidelines_, etc.. These can then be referenced and adopted by the other epics. If left inside T102A, T102A's deliverables would become bloated and coupling would increase (T102B and T102C would have to reach into T102A's outputs). Separation aligns with **separation of concerns** principle: T102A improves SPS content, T102E improves the research process feeding all content.
- **Threshold of Effort vs Benefit:** Using an **effort/impact matrix**, we consider that splitting into a new epic has some overhead (writing separate plans, managing another backlog), but the impact of doing so is high (ensuring robust implementation of all research governance elements). The risk of _not_ splitting is that the effort required for these governance changes might be underestimated or under-resourced if lumped with SPS template tasks (since those tend to be more concrete and might overshadow the intangible process stuff). An epic designation elevates its importance. The organization can then allocate dedicated capacity (maybe specific sprint goals around research workflow, separate from SPS template sprints). Also, the epic can be tracked to closure when all governance elements are done, giving a clear Done criteria.
- **Industry Patterns:** In methodologies like PRINCE2, something akin to this would be handled as a separate _Work Package_ or even a _Project_ if it's big enough, because it's a _quality/process improvement_ endeavor. Agile frameworks (SAFe) separate Business Epics from Enabler Epics. This is clearly an Enabler (it doesn't deliver direct end-user functionality, but improves the process/tooling for delivering functionality). SAFe recommends that enablers follow the same lifecycle but be recognized and approved separately, often with their own Lean Business Case. If we treat research workflow formalization as an enabler epic, we can create a Lean Business Case that justifies it (in fact, our research here serves as that justification - it identifies a significant bottleneck and quality gap that warrants investment).
- **Scalability and Future Use:** If these research governance mechanisms prove valuable, they might be adopted beyond T102 (other initiatives that use LLM_Consultant). Having it as a separate epic makes it easier to reference and reuse in documentation - e.g., "See Epic T102E for the standard research workflow - apply those outputs here." If it's buried in T102A, future teams might not notice it as a general solution. So for the sake of reusability and clarity, separate epic packaging is beneficial.

### Why Not Integrate into T102A (Option B)?

T102A's primary goal is implementing the SPS workflow and template. While some overlap exists (especially for SPS-specific research integration rules), most of the heavy lifting for research governance is orthogonal. Integrating into T102A would mean that the SPS template story and SPS guideline story would now balloon to also cover creation of new templates and cross-epic processes. The team would have to juggle two types of work: content creation for SPS vs framework creation for research. This could dilute focus and also complicate T102A's acceptance criteria. T102A success should be measured by a robust SPS artifact; adding "and also fix the whole research process" risks partial completion or trade-offs.

Additionally, some deliverables (like LLM quality control framework) are beyond the scope of SPS itself - they apply to how we do research generally (e.g., also when feeding into Concept stage decisions). So shoehorning that in SPS epic isn't logical from a content perspective. It would likely produce a less organized result (bits of procedure buried in SPS docs rather than in a dedicated location).

The only advantage of Option B (integrating into T102A) is possibly shorter timeline or simpler management (one less epic to track). If the organization were strapped for overhead, they might want to just treat it as "more stories under T102A". However, given the criticality flagged (the research issue was marked **HIGH criticality**), it deserves first-class treatment.

We did consider **Option C: Separate Initiative** if the scope seemed to affect multiple layers enterprise-wide (for example, an AI governance initiative that goes beyond consultancy). But at present, the scope is within improving the LLM_Consultant's process in delivering SPS/Request/Concept. It doesn't yet warrant a whole new initiative (which might be numbered differently). Option C might be chosen if, say, the company decided to spin off a project to build an internal "LLM Research Governance Framework" product for all LLM use cases across roles - that would go beyond T102's charter. Since our charge is within T102, making it an epic (T102E) is appropriate and sufficient.

We can present an **effort-impact matrix** simplistically:

- Option A (New Epic): Effort (medium) - manage separate backlog, some coordination; Impact (high) - high focus yields high-quality outputs, broad adoption.
- Option B (Within T102A): Effort (medium maybe) - combined with other tasks, could be lost; Impact (medium) - improvements might only partially realized, might rush or scope-cut if time tight.
- Option C (Separate Initiative): Effort (high) - overhead of new initiative, maybe not justified by scope; Impact (potentially high but mostly same outputs as epic, just more bureaucratic weight behind it).

Thus, Option A emerges as the **balanced choice with maximum benefit**.

### Implementation via Epic T102E

If approved, we would formulate Epic T102E: _"Research Workflow & Governance Formalization"_. The epic would contain features such as: - **RBST (Research Brief Structural Template)** - creation of the template artifact (with versioning, etc.). - **RRST (Research Report Structural Template)** - likewise for the report structure. - **RWPG (Research Workflow Procedural Guidelines)** - updates to SPSPG, Request PG, Concept PG, etc., or a new consolidated guideline that covers commissioning, validation, integration of research. - **QLLM (Quality controls for LLM Research)** - this could be a feature capturing the development of checklists, logging procedures, and possibly tooling support (e.g., setting up a log repository or updating the prompt engineering standards for LLM_Researcher). - Possibly any needed **training or documentation** for team members on the new process.

We would also create GDRs from the research (like T102-GDR-006, T102-GDR-007) as part of this epic's outputs, because establishing those governance rules is a deliverable on its own.

It's important to schedule this epic appropriately. Since T102A (SPS) is already underway, we might implement some quick fixes in T102A immediately (especially for any urgent SPS deliverable issues), but the fuller effort will run as T102E possibly parallel to finishing T102A/B. To ensure coordination: T102E will interface with T102A, T102B, T102C leads to implement changes in those artifacts. But as an epic owner, one can manage that centrally.

**Conclusion:** We will pursue **Option A - a dedicated Epic for Research Workflow Governance** (Deliverable D) as it offers a structured and focused approach to implement the recommended changes effectively without interfering with the ongoing product-centric epics. This approach is consistent with agile best practices for handling sizable enabling work and will yield a more maintainable outcome.

### Phase Sequencing and Rollout Plan

**Phase 1: Immediate Quick Wins (Weeks 0-1):** Since T102A is underway, implement some low-effort, high-impact changes at once: - Start using the Research Brief/Report templates in draft form for any new research commissioned now (even before formal approval). These steps address urgent issues (like document bloat risk). For instance, if SPS was stuck because new issues kept arising (as in our research brief description of the governance freeze problem), implementing Gate 1 and Gate 2 concept immediately might help progress.

**Phase 2: Develop and Approve Artifacts (Weeks 1-3):** This is where the bulk of new artifact creation happens: - Draft the GDRs (006, 003, 007) and circulate for approval. Because these are governance, getting client sign-off early ensures top-level buy-in. - Create templates (RBST, RRST) in collaboration with the consulting team, maybe review them with a small pilot (like fill one out fictitiously or with a past example to ensure they work). - Update the procedural guidelines. This might involve writing new sections and then having the client or decision owner review them to ensure they align with expectations and don't conflict with existing company policy. - Meanwhile, the separate epic T102E would be formally launched and stories entered into the tracking system (Jira or similar), which is more an administrative step but helps keep work structured. If needed, secure resources (maybe assign the LLM_Researcher role partially to this epic's tasks for a sprint).

**Phase 3: Training & Tooling (Week 3-4):** Once the processes and templates are documented, hold a short training/workshop with all LLM_Consultant team members (and any other stakeholders like client reps if they are heavily involved in approvals). Walk through a scenario using the new workflow. Address questions. This builds confidence and readiness. - If any tooling needs adjustment (for instance, setting up a Confluence space for research reports or updating a repository structure), do it now. Perhaps also implement a simple reference numbering scheme (maybe maintain a JSON/YAML file mapping research IDs to links, if we want automation for inserting references). - Ensure the LLM itself is configured to help: e.g., if we have a system prompt or style for ChatGPT to always answer with citations or in a certain format, update that.

**Phase 4: Pilot and Feedback (Week 4-6):** Use the new workflow on a real small-scale case. Perhaps commission a new research piece on a non-critical question to see how smoothly the brief → research → report → integration flows. Solicit feedback from the person who did it: was the template clear, did the process feel cumbersome or smooth? Iron out any hiccups (maybe the brief template needed an additional section or the verification checklist missed something). - Meanwhile continue migrating existing content: convert older notes and retrofit the SPS and any in-progress Request/Concept docs to the new style to ensure uniformity.

**Phase 5: Full Rollout and Monitor (Week 6+):** Declare the new system live for all future research needs on T102 and related. At this point, the epic can be marked done when all deliverables are delivered. But governance is an ongoing activity: monitor how it's working. - Put on the agenda of the next project retrospective to discuss the LLM research process - capture any improvement suggestions. - Possibly plan an audit a few months out: pick one research integrated and trace it to verify compliance (did we find the log, were all sources present?). This can be done by QA or PMO to ensure the governance is truly being followed, not just written.

**Ownership Model:** - During implementation, a **Research Workflow Lead** (could be the person who conducted this research, i.e., the LLM_Researcher in a specialist capacity, or the initiative's QA lead) should drive it. They coordinate creating docs, getting approvals, and training. The **Client (Decision Owner)** will sign off on GDRs, meaning they implicitly sponsor this change - that's important because it gives authority to enforce these rules. - Post-implementation, adherence becomes everyone's responsibility, but practically the **LLM_Consultant lead** on each epic will be responsible for ensuring their team follows the research process (just as they'd ensure other processes are followed). The **client or PMO** might also periodically check that the governance is being adhered to (especially for the AI governance parts, if there is sensitivity around that). - If any new questions about the process arise or modifications needed, those should go through the established governance change management (e.g., update the GDR or issue a new one if we adjust a threshold or add a new tool).

By structuring the rollout in phases and clearly assigning ownership (who writes templates, who approves, who trains, who monitors), we maximize the likelihood of adoption success. The changes should transition from theoretical (report recommendations) to practiced behavior. Since many of these improvements directly address pain points the team has felt (like too much note-writing, uncertainty about AI content), we expect good buy-in.

We will also document the "changelog" of these governance improvements in the SPS's own Change Log section, so there's a historical record in the project artifact that, e.g., on Oct 5, 2025, research integration standard v1 was adopted. This is useful for future reference.

In conclusion, implementation will be carried out systematically: establishing firm governance rules (the "what/why"), detailed guidelines and templates (the "how"), and ensuring the team is equipped to execute (training and oversight). The phased approach ensures that critical pieces (like preventing further document bloat or unchecked AI output) are addressed immediately, while the fuller framework is rolled out thoughtfully. By end of this rollout, T102 should have a **repeatable, auditable research workflow** ingrained in its operations, and a blueprint for other initiatives to follow.
