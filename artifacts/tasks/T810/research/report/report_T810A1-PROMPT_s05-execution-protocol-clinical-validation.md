# Execution Protocol Validation - Clinical and Prompt Patterns

## Domain A: Clinical Gastroenterology Best Practices

**Structured Consultation Frameworks:** Gastroenterologists structure consultations in phases that mirror a logical flow from open-ended inquiry to collaborative planning. An initial **open question** invites the patient's narrative (e.g., "What brought you here today?") before drilling into specifics. This stage emphasizes _active listening_ - physicians avoid interrupting or rushing to solutions, ensuring the patient's full agenda surfaces. Classic prompts include, _"What do you think you have? What worries you the most about these symptoms? What do you hope I can do for you?"_ - these invite the patient's perspective, fears, and expectations. Only after understanding the patient's viewpoint do experts begin systematic symptom inquiry (history of present illness), covering GI-specific details like stool frequency/form, pain triggers, diet, etc., often using standardized tools. For example, the **Bristol Stool Form Scale** is routinely used to categorize stool consistency into Types 1-7, providing a common language for bowel habits. This structured yet patient-centric history prevents missing key data while maintaining rapport.

**Expert Questioning & Probing Techniques:** GI clinicians employ a funnel approach - start broad, then probe deeper based on initial answers. They use the patient's own words to formulate clarifying questions, avoiding medical jargon until necessary. Open-ended **Socratic questioning** uncovers not just symptoms but context ("Can you walk me through a typical day of meals and symptoms?"). Clinicians also use motivational interviewing (MI) techniques: **OARS** (Open questions, Affirmations, Reflective listening, Summaries) to encourage the patient to elaborate and to validate their efforts. For instance, a doctor might say, _"It sounds like you feel your diet might be triggering symptoms - tell me more about that"_, which is open-ended and validates the patient's insight. **Reflections** and **empathic acknowledgments** are interwoven with questions: _"I can imagine how frustrating it is to deal with this pain every day"_, or _"It seems you're worried this could be something serious, is that right?"_ Such statements both acknowledge emotions and double-check understanding, creating a dialogue rather than an interrogation. This aligns with patient-centered frameworks like Motivational Interviewing, where **open questions** and empathic reflections build trust. Importantly, experts avoid rapid-fire questioning; they pause to **respond to patient cues**. If a patient mentions stress or anxiety, an adept gastroenterologist will probe that link (_"You mentioned stress - do you notice your stomach worse on stressful days?"_) and respond with empathy before moving on. This adaptive probing prevents the encounter from feeling like a checklist and instead like a conversation guided by the patient's responses.

**Patient-Centered Communication & Uncertainty Handling:** Effective GI consultations are rooted in _"relationship-centered care,"_ treating the patient as a partner in decision-making. Clinicians explicitly **validate** patients' experiences - even if symptoms lack a clear organic cause - to build trust. For example, a physician might say, _"I know people have told you 'it's just stress', but I want you to know I believe your symptoms are real"_. This validation addresses a common breakdown: IBS patients often feel dismissed; guidelines urge providers to **provide a confident, positive diagnosis and affirm that IBS is a real condition** to legitimize the patient's illness experience. When discussing findings and next steps, gastroenterologists use **transparent yet reassuring language**. They **express uncertainty honestly** but optimistically - an approach sometimes called _"therapeutic uncertainty."_ Rather than a blunt "I don't know what's wrong," a GI doctor might say, _"So far all tests look normal, which is good - it means no serious disease. Your symptoms are most consistent with IBS, but we'll keep an eye out for any red flags. Let's work together on managing this, and we can adjust if new clues appear."_ This kind of phrasing acknowledges uncertainty and frames it as a shared problem to solve, often coupled with reassurance (_"nothing life-threatening is evident"_) and an invitation for the patient's input or questions. Studies on communication in IBS highlight that **avoiding false reassurance** is key - e.g. **never saying "Don't worry, it's nothing"** which can sound dismissive. Instead, good communicators **address the patient's specific fears** (_"You were concerned about cancer; given your results, I'm confident we're not seeing signs of that"_) and follow with what is known and what the plan is, to instill confidence. They also set **realistic expectations** if a cure is unlikely: _"I understand you want these symptoms gone immediately. Because this is a chronic issue, my goal is to reduce them significantly over the next few months. Would a 30% improvement in pain be a good start?"_. Such collaborative goal-setting aligns the plan with patient expectations and invites agreement.

**Empathy and Motivational Framing:** Throughout the consult, effective GI practitioners use **empathic and motivational language**. They deploy the **NURSE** technique for empathy - Name the emotion (_"I can see you're frustrated"_), express Understanding (_"Anyone in your position would feel that way"_), show Respect (_"You've been really strong in managing this so far"_), offer Support (_"I'm here to help you through this"_), and **Explore** further (_"Could you tell me more about how this affects your daily life?"_). In gastroenterology, where symptoms like bowel habits can be embarrassing, **non-judgmental tone** is critical. Phrases like _"Some people feel shy talking about bowel movements, but this is a normal part of health - please feel free to be open"_ set a safe atmosphere. Clinicians also use **Motivational Interviewing (MI)** for lifestyle coaching (diet, stress management). Rather than dictate changes, they ask _"How ready do you feel to try a new diet approach?"_ or _"What changes do you think would be most doable for you?"_, eliciting the patient's own motivations. This patient-centered style has been shown to improve engagement and outcomes in IBS by fostering a therapeutic alliance. It contrasts with authoritarian advice-giving; the physician becomes a coach who _guides_ the patient to choose feasible changes (e.g. exploring a low FODMAP diet if the patient is willing). Throughout, **positive reinforcement** is given for any progress or insight the patient shares (e.g., _"That's a great observation that dairy upsets you - that clue is very helpful!"_).

**Common Communication Pitfalls (Anti-Patterns):** The literature documents several communication breakdowns to avoid. One is a **rapid interrogation** style - firing off a series of closed questions without responding to the patient's answers. This "checklist" approach makes patients feel like "a set of symptoms" rather than a person, and they may shut down. Best practice is to acknowledge each answer ("I see, thanks for telling me that") or follow it with a related empathy statement before the next question. Another anti-pattern is **premature reassurance or minimization** - e.g., immediately saying "It's probably nothing serious" before a proper evaluation, or "Just relax, you'll be fine." Patients interpret this as dismissive. Instead, effective communicators first **acknowledge the severity** of what the patient experiences (_"I understand this pain is disrupting your life"_) before offering reassurance based on evidence (_"Your labs are normal, which is a good sign - let's talk about what we can do for the pain."_). A related pitfall is **focusing solely on biomedical facts** while ignoring psychosocial aspects. For instance, telling an IBS patient to "change your diet and exercise" without addressing their anxiety about symptoms or the impact on their life misses crucial elements and can damage trust. **Jargon and technical language** pose another barrier - saying "functional bowel disorder" without explanation can confuse or alienate patients. Good practice is to use simple language first (_"Your digestive system is sensitive, kind of like a nervous stomach"_) and only introduce medical terms with explanation (**teach-back** can be used: _"Can you tell me in your own words what IBS is, so I know we're on the same page?"_). Finally, **lack of patient involvement** is a major no-go: making a treatment plan without checking the patient's willingness or understanding often leads to non-adherence. Experts instead _share decisions_, offering choices (_"We have a couple of options - we could try a daily medication or start with diet changes. What are your thoughts?"_) and guiding the patient to a mutual decision. In sum, positive patterns revolve around **asking before telling, validating throughout, and partnering** with the patient, whereas negative patterns involve telling without asking, dismissing concerns, or treating the interaction as a one-way flow.

## Domain B: LLM Agent Prompt Patterning (LLM_Gastro)

**LLM_Consultant "General" Prompt Protocol:** The LLM_Gastro system's consultant agent is explicitly programmed to follow a disciplined, multi-phase consultation protocol that echoes clinical best practices. In the **"Consultant | General" system prompt**, the AI is instructed to act as a _"Senior Technical Consultant"_ with a **Socratic, inquisitive approach**. The prompt defines a strict **Execution Protocol** with sequential phases that the LLM _"MUST follow ... precisely"_, effectively encoding guardrails against premature answers. For example, **Phase 0: Intake & Framing** requires the AI to first issue a **disclaimer** and then produce an **"Initial Framing Echo"** - a concise summary of the user's request - followed by asking for confirmation: _"Does that sound right as a starting point?"_. The agent **cannot proceed until the user explicitly approves Gate A** (framing agreement). This gatekeeping ensures the AI does not move forward without client alignment, mirroring how a human consultant or physician first confirms the chief complaint and understanding. If the user says "No," the prompt specifies a **Reframing Loop** sub-protocol to try again, and only after two failures does it escalate the issue (similar to a doctor re-phrasing and, if still misunderstood, seeking supervisory help).

In **Phase 1: Discover & Define**, the LLM_Consultant must engage in **Socratic inquiry** - the prompt explicitly instructs the agent to _"ask broad, open-ended questions"_ and then converge with more specific questions, **always maintaining a Socratic stance (asking rather than telling)**. The AI is even directed to state working assumptions and invite correction (e.g., "It sounds like X is the main issue, is that right?") as it narrows the problem definition. Notably, the prompt places a _hard prohibition_ on giving solutions in this phase: **"NEVER propose solutions prematurely"** - no matter how obvious - _"until Gate B has been passed with explicit client approval."_. This is a direct guardrail against the common AI tendency to jump into answer mode; it forces a **probe-first behavior**. The consultant AI must explicitly synthesize a **Problem Statement** and get the user's approval on it ("lock in" the problem) before any advice. The phrasing example given: _"Does this accurately capture the core challenge we need to solve?"_ and an instruction: \*_"DO NOT proceed to Phase 2 until you receive explicit approval."_. This effectively enforces the two-step confirmation akin to a doctor obtaining patient buy-in on the diagnosis/problem list before moving to management.

In **Phase 2: Develop & Deliver**, the LLM_Consultant is similarly constrained to a structured process. It must first help establish **decision criteria** with the user, then generate **multiple solution options** (specifically **"2-4 distinct high-level solutions, including a 'do-nothing' baseline"** option). This requirement to always include a "Status Quo" option is a guardrail ensuring the AI doesn't railroad the user into doing something - it's akin to a clinician saying "One option is we change nothing for now," to ensure balanced decision-making. The AI then must present a **comparison table** of options and make a **transparent recommendation** (narrative reasoning for the best choice), but even here it must phrase it _as guidance, not final truth_ (e.g., _"Approach 2 seems best because… How does this align with your view?"_). Finally, the agent must get explicit user confirmation on the chosen solution (Gate B, the _"Conceptual Agreement Gate"_ - e.g., _"Are we agreed to proceed with X solution?"_). Only with that go-ahead can it proceed to Phase 3. This design mirrors shared decision-making: the AI is forced to **present options and ask for the user's decision**, rather than unilaterally solving the problem.

Phase 3 then handles closure: the prompt has the AI **summarize the outcomes and request a satisfaction rating**, ensuring it checks the user's perspective at the end (just as a doctor might ask, "Does this plan sound okay to you?" and gauge the patient's comfort). The entire prompt is imbued with **behavioral guardrails** that echo clinical communication values. For instance, under **Core Principles**, the system states the agent must _"Maintain a Socratic Stance - ask questions, don't provide answers (especially in Phase 1)"_ and _"Embody the Advisor Persona - you are an expert partner, not an order-taker, so challenge unclear requests"_. These instructions ensure the AI adopts a **collegial, guiding tone** rather than the default overly accommodating "yes-man" persona. Also, the prompt explicitly flags certain assistant behaviors to **prohibit**: e.g., the AI should not slip into a service-oriented style offering to perform tasks or provide extra help outside the consult protocol (no "Would you like me to do X for you?" proposals - a known pattern of ChatGPT's assistant persona that the prompt aims to suppress). Instead, the agent stays in an analyst mindset, focusing on asking and validating until the gates allow moving forward.

**Guardrails and Escalation Triggers:** The LLM_Consultant prompt includes specific **escalation protocols** for stuck scenarios, similar to a clinician knowing when to call for help. One is the **"\[5-ROUND CAP\]"**: if 5 back-and-forth exchanges in a phase don't achieve alignment, the AI must escalate by saying _"It seems we're having trouble aligning. Could you help me rephrase the core objective from your perspective?"_. This is effectively a graceful fallback to avoid an infinite loop - it signals a reset, much as a doctor might pause a circular conversation and ask the patient to describe their main goal in their own words. Another is the **"\[KNOWLEDGE GAP\]"** trigger: if the user's query needs specialized expertise beyond the AI (e.g., complex medical content outside its scope), the prompt instructs the AI to recommend consulting a human expert. The given phrasing: _"I recommend consulting a human specialist in \[domain\]. Shall I note that in our plan?"_. This guardrail not only prevents the AI from guessing or hallucinating an answer it shouldn't give, but it also **mirrors real clinical caution** (knowing when to refer to a specialist). Additionally, the prompt defines **"Hard Gate" refusal messages** if the user tries to skip ahead. For example, if the user asks for a solution before agreeing on the problem (Gate A not passed), the AI should refuse with: _"Cannot proceed with discovery until initial framing agreed. Please confirm understanding and check Gate A."_. These pre-written refusal lines act like safety bumpers to keep the conversation on the intended track.

**VPA Agent (LLM_Trader) Conditional Workflow:** In contrast to the conversational Socratic style of the consultant, the **VPA (Volume-Price Analysis) trading agent** prompt (main_v2.) shows a more algorithmic, multi-step **execution pattern** tailored to a specific workflow. The VPA agent is tasked with _"enforcing disciplined execution of high-probability \[trading\] setups"_ - effectively a coach that prevents impulsive actions. Its system prompt defines a strict **"PRIORITY SEQUENCE (ABSOLUTE)"** for every response, listing out numbered sections that must appear in order. This sequence is: **1\. TTI (Timeframe Trend Identification)**, 2. **CAS (Context Analysis)**, 3. **SPP (Strategic Planning Protocol)**, 4. **TEA (Trade Execution Assessment)**, 5. **TMI (Trade Management Identification)**, then **Devil's Advocate** and **TLDR with Action Plan**. The design is essentially a template the agent must fill, with some sections optional based on context (marked "optional" in the list). Each section has well-defined **trigger conditions** and **guard rules**. For example, **TTI** will run _"IF trading plan is provided"_ or updated if data is provided; if required input data is missing or unreadable, the agent must output _"CANNOT COMPLETE TTI: \[reason\]"_ **and STOP** the sequence. This is a built-in guardrail to prevent the agent from making up analysis - it would rather halt than produce a potentially flawed output without necessary data (analogous to a doctor saying "I can't interpret these results without the lab data - we need that first").

The **conditional triggers** in VPA show a pattern of **protocol gating based on input type**, much like what LLM_Gastro needs for different user inputs. For instance, the VPA's **SPP (planning)** only runs _"when the user explicitly asks to rerun the plan"_. A **guard clause** is given: _"Never rerun SPP without user request; always use the latest available values from the prior run."_. This prevents redundant or unprompted planning - a parallel in LLM_Gastro might be not to re-analyze or re-summarize tracking data unless new input arrives or user asks. Another example: **TEA (Trade Execution Assessment)** triggers only if the user asks something like "Should I execute this trade now?". It even has an advisory guard: _If a "major regime shift" just occurred (e.g., breaking news),_ _suggest re-running SPP first_ **before doing execution analysis**[**\[31\]**]**. This is a nuanced conditional: the agent should effectively say "Conditions changed, let's redo the plan step before acting" - akin to an AI doctor agent saying "Given this new symptom, let's re-evaluate our analysis before giving advice." It highlights how the VPA agent uses** internal gating logic **to decide which sub-protocols to invoke in response to the query. The LLM_Gastro system can draw on this pattern to implement its own** input-type detection\*\*: e.g., if a user input contains only new tracking data and the word "UPDATE," trigger a short tracking-update protocol vs. if the input is an open narrative, trigger the full consult.

**Multi-Phase Execution & Enforcement:** The VPA agent's output is highly structured. It must produce multiple sections in sequence every time, ensuring **no steps are skipped**. This is enforced by the prompt's layered design (Layer 1: Protocols, Layer 2: Analysis framework, etc.). The agent doesn't wait for user approval between these sections - instead, it outputs a comprehensive analysis report each time. However, it has built-in **gates in content**: for example, in **TEA**, after grading the trade setup, the rules say: _"Execute only if Overall grade ≥ B-; otherwise WAIT and state what would improve the setup."_. So if conditions are not favorable (the agent's analysis yields a C grade, for instance), it will explicitly advise to hold off ("Verdict: Wait") and explain why. This is analogous to a medical agent having a threshold for action - e.g., "only give a treatment recommendation if confidence is high; if confidence < X, prompt for verification or defer decision." Indeed, LLM_Gastro aims for a **"trust-and-verify" workflow**, similar to how the VPA uses confidence grades (A/B/C) to decide whether to execute or not. The VPA also always includes a **"Devil's Advocate"** step (item 6 in the sequence) which presumably forces the agent to consider an opposing scenario or risk (e.g., highlighting what could go wrong or an alternative interpretation), and a **"TL;DR with Action Plan"** summary at the end for quick takeaway. These are additional guardrails to ensure the output isn't one-sided - the Devil's Advocate injects a last-moment self-skeptical check, and the TL;DR ensures the advice is digestible. By comparison, the LLM_Consultant doesn't have a formal "Devil's Advocate" step in its general prompt, but it does emphasize transparency about options and asking the user if the recommendation aligns with them, which serves a similar purpose of not over-indexing on the AI's own suggestion.

**Differences Between LLM_Consultant and VPA Enforcement:** The LLM_Consultant's prompt patterns are centered on **interactive dialogue enforcement** - it is all about _when_ to ask, _when_ to answer, and obtaining user input at critical junctures (gates). It explicitly forbids certain assistant behaviors (like giving unsolicited help or skipping to solutions) to maintain a Socratic consultant persona. The **tone is conversational**: even the guardrails (e.g., refusal messages at gates) are phrased in a polite explanatory way. In contrast, the VPA agent's patterns are about **output structure and content gating**. It doesn't ask the user for permission between steps; instead, it internally decides which sections to output based on triggers. The VPA's enforcement is more _algorithmic_: it has if-then rules and stops or includes sections accordingly, and it uses internal thresholds (grades, triggers) to govern recommendations. The consultant agent uses _soft enforcement_ via dialogue (e.g., asking for confirmation serves as enforcement of understanding), whereas the VPA uses _hard enforcement_ via template and if-else logic (e.g., refusing to produce a plan without a request, or halting analysis if data is missing). Another difference is in **feedback style**: the consultant agent continuously asks the user for feedback (confirmation, agreement, rating), essentially enforcing a feedback loop. The VPA agent provides feedback in the form of analytical justifications (e.g., "Verdict: Wait - mid-range, no bullish trigger" in a table) but it's not asking the user questions; it's delivering a verdict. This reflects their roles: one is a collaborative consultant, the other is a semi-automated analyst.

Despite these differences, both share a commitment to **protocol fidelity**. Both have **explicit rules to prevent premature action**. The consultant must not give advice early (no jumping to Phase 2), and the VPA must not give an execution recommendation if conditions aren't met (it will say "WAIT" and specify what's missing). Both also integrate **verification steps**: the consultant via user gates, the VPA via re-check triggers and Devil's Advocate. Notably, both agents explicitly check for **user prompts indicating a certain mode**. The LLM_Consultant would presumably detect if a user prompt is a straightforward question unrelated to the ongoing consult (and might need to break protocol to answer or trigger a different flow - though the prompt as given is for a general consult scenario). The VPA clearly parses the user request: if the user just shares data vs asks a question vs requests a plan, different subsets of the sequence run. This conditional pattern is something the LLM_Gastro prompts can adopt: for example, a **"tracking-only update"** input could trigger just a JSON update and minimal response, whereas a **narrative symptom story** triggers the full Engage→Analyze→Probe workflow, and an explicit **advice request** might trigger a Coach phase. The repository confirms LLM_Gastro will implement similar logic (e.g., NFR-008 Protocol Triggering Intelligence) drawing on VPA-style input parsing.

**Prompt Phrasing Examples for Guardrails:** Both files provide concrete examples of how the AI should phrase its adherence to protocol. For gatekeeping, the consultant prompt has lines like: _"--> GATE A: Await Framing Approval."_ and if not approved, a refusal such as _"Cannot proceed with discovery until initial framing agreed…"_. For probe enforcement, we see directives like _"ask broad questions… DO NOT proceed… without explicit approval"_. The VPA's guardrail phrasing is evident in lines like _"If data is missing → State 'CANNOT COMPLETE TTI: \[reason\]' and STOP."_, or _"Never rerun SPP without user request"_. These exact phrases can be reused or adapted in LLM_Gastro's prompt to ensure the model follows the intended logic (for instance, incorporating a rule: _If user has not provided new info but asks for an update, respond with a gentle refusal or prompt for info_). Another useful phrasing is the consultant's escalation: _"It seems we're having trouble aligning. To make sure we're on the right track, could you help me rephrase…?"_ - this polite, collaborative tone for handling impasses is a positive pattern to emulate.

## Cross-Domain Synthesis: Integrating Clinical and AI Protocol Patterns

The intersection of clinical communication best practices and LLM prompt engineering reveals **complementary patterns**. Key observations and exemplar phrases include:

- **Gatekeeping & Phase Transitions:** Both domains stress _"don't jump ahead"_. Clinically, physicians ensure mutual understanding before moving to planning; the LLM prompt enforces the same via explicit gates. A positive example: _Doctor_: "Let me summarize what I've heard. \[Summarizes\]. Did I get that right?"; **LLM**: "Based on our conversation, here's the problem statement I've synthesized. Does this accurately capture the core issue?". Both prompt an explicit **yes/no confirmation**. The rationale is to avoid proceeding on false assumptions. An anti-pattern in either context is barreling forward: e.g. a doctor launching into treatment options before the patient agrees what the problem is, or an AI giving a solution without confirming it understood the question - both can lead to confusion or misalignment. The enforced gates in LLM_Gastro mimic the **"teach-back"** method doctors use, effectively embedding it into the AI's protocol.
- **Socratic Probing vs. Premature Solutions:** Domain A encourages **analysis-through-questions** (e.g., using questions to guide the patient to insight and gather info) rather than offering quick answers. Domain B encodes this via rules like _"Maintain Socratic stance - ask clarifying questions, don't provide answers in Phase 1."_. A sample consultant AI prompt line exemplifying this is, _"You always seek to understand more deeply before concluding."_. In practice, a gastroenterologist might say, _"Before I give my thoughts, I have a few more questions about your symptoms,"_ instead of prematurely saying _"You probably have IBS, try a fiber supplement."_ The AI similarly withholds any "here's what you should do" until after thorough probing. The **anti-pattern** is the "fix-it reflex" - e.g., an AI that responds to _"My stomach hurts"_ with _"I'm sorry to hear that. Would you like me to suggest some remedies?"_ on the very first reply. That mirrors a doctor simply handing a prescription without investigation. Both are frowned upon. The guardrails explicitly ban that reflex: _"Forbidden until Gate B…"_. In blending domains, we see the concept of **two-loop minimum**: the AI must ask at least one round of questions before offering advice, akin to the medical norm of **assess before you advise**.
- **Empathic Tone and Acknowledgment:** The clinical approach prizes empathic phrasing and validating patient feelings. While an LLM lacks genuine emotion, its prompt can enforce empathic language patterns. For instance, a clinician might respond to a symptom report with _"That sounds very distressing. I can understand how hard that must be."_ The LLM_Consultant, in its examples, similarly uses **empathetic acknowledgments** within its dialogue exemplars: e.g., _"Thank you for that context. To help me narrow this down…"_ - the "thank you" acknowledges the user's input, showing appreciation just as a doctor would. Another example: _"I can see how this would be difficult to manage"_ could be baked into the AI's responses when patients describe tough symptoms. The prompt doesn't list specific empathy phrases, but it does emphasize _"Client-Centric Framing"_ and a collaborative style. Designers can incorporate known clinical empathy phrases into the prompt's tone guidelines. A notable cross-over pattern is **validation of concerns**: doctors explicitly validate ("Your concern about X is completely understandable"). In AI, this could manifest as the agent saying, _"It's completely valid to be concerned about these symptoms - many people would feel that way."_ Such phrases reassure the user and build trust. The **anti-pattern** on both sides is dismissiveness. If an AI were to say _"Don't worry, I'm sure it's fine"_ without basis, it maps to the false reassurance doctors avoid. Both domains show that **acknowledging uncertainty and concern, rather than sweeping it aside, is crucial**. LLM prompts can borrow medical phrases like _"We'll figure this out together"_ or _"Let's keep an eye on that, I share your concern and we won't ignore it."_
- **Differential Diagnosis & Epistemic Hedging:** In clinical practice, especially for uncertain conditions, experts often discuss multiple possibilities (the _differential diagnosis_) and hedge their statements (using phrases like "it could be X or Y," "likely vs unlikely," "we can't be certain yet"). This communicates honesty and caution. In the AI prompt domain, **epistemic hedging** is encouraged to avoid AI overconfidence. For example, LLM_Gastro might be prompted to phrase analyses with uncertainty indicators: _"The pattern_ _suggests_ _a possible sensitivity to dairy, though I'm_ _not 100% certain"_, which parallels a doctor saying, "It's **likely** lactose intolerance, but we should test to confirm." By design, the LLM_Consultant is more about problem-solving than diagnosing an illness, but it does include lines like presenting findings based on **confidence levels** and asking for verification when confidence is low (these appear in the research plan for trust-and-verify). The VPA agent actually operationalizes numeric confidence: it uses letter grades (A, B, C, D) to quantify setup strength and acts only above a threshold. LLM_Gastro can combine these approaches: qualitatively describe confidence (e.g., _"I'm fairly confident this symptom is diet-related, but I'd like to confirm a bit more"_) and implement triggers (if confidence <70%, perhaps prompt the user for more info or suggest waiting). A cross-domain example of hedging: _Doctor_: "I think stress might be contributing to your symptoms, although we can't say it's the only cause." **AI**: "Your description makes me **wonder if** stress could be a trigger - it's a possibility we should consider, though I'd like to gather more evidence." This careful wording avoids definitive claims, aligning with both medical prudence and AI alignment (preventing overstatement).
- **Protocol Triggering & Context Awareness:** The necessity for the AI to choose the right "mode" based on input is directly informed by clinical workflow triage. A gastroenterologist mentally distinguishes: _Is this visit just a quick follow-up on a single issue, a routine tracking update, or a new complex consult?_ They adjust their approach accordingly (e.g., a quick check-in might skip re-taking full history and focus only on new developments). Similarly, LLM_Gastro's prompt can integrate **input parsing** heuristics akin to the VPA's triggers. Cross-domain, one can create a mapping: if user input looks like structured data or an update ("Here is today's log: …"), the agent should perhaps skip the full Engage→Probe protocol and instead do a brief **analysis + summary update** (no extensive probing unless something is abnormal). If input is a direct question ("What foods should I avoid?"), maybe trigger a **Coaching** response (Phase 2.5) even if it's out of the typical sequence - much like a patient explicitly asking for advice triggers a doctor to give recommendations sooner. The VPA's design - _"always run context analysis";_ "never run planning unless asked"; _"suggest rerun planning if context changed"_ - provides a blueprint. For LLM_Gastro, one rule might be: **If user input contains JSON data update and no explicit question, assume it's a tracking-only update → skip directly to analysis phase and brief probe for missing fields**. Indeed the research notes mention **"Tracking-Only Trigger: imperative keywords (e.g., 'UPDATE the JSON…')"** and **"Full Protocol Trigger: narrative note + images without tracking keywords"**, etc. This logic is directly analogous to the VPA's triggers and can be implemented in the prompt with instructions or examples.
- **Feedback and Iterative Loop Enforcement:** A salient cross-domain pattern is enforcing a **feedback loop** before final advice. In medicine, this is the iterative process of gathering info, checking understanding, and only then reaching conclusions - often requiring more than one patient encounter or at least multiple question-answer exchanges in one visit. LLM_Consultant's prompt explicitly forces at least one full Q&A loop (Engage→Probe) before any Summarize or Coach. For example, after initial analysis, the AI might be prompted to always ask, _"Does this interpretation seem accurate to you?"_ or, _"Is there anything I missed?"_, before it goes on to give recommendations. This ensures the **patient/user gets to correct or add info**. The prompt already does this at major gates (Problem Alignment Gate, etc.), but cross-domain insight suggests even minor verifications improve outcomes (akin to a doctor saying "Let me double-check I've got everything before we move on"). The VPA's approach to iteration is less about user input (since it's mostly one-turn answers), but it does have a notion of **monitoring and Next Check** - e.g., after giving an execution plan, it says what to watch for next and what to do if conditions change. LLM_Gastro can borrow this idea by ending sessions with guidance on what the patient should monitor and report next time (e.g., _"Try eliminating dairy for a week and note any change; we'll review this data in our next session."_). This provides a continuous loop akin to a doctor's follow-up plan and encourages patient engagement in tracking - bridging to the **tracking artifacts (Cara Care style diaries)** that the system uses. Indeed, the prompt resources include a gastro_example_tracking.json and screenshots of Cara Care meal/symptom tracking, indicating the system will incorporate diary data. The cross-domain principle is that **closing the loop with concrete next steps** (either instructing what to do or what to observe) is a positive pattern. The anti-patterns for the AI (e.g., immediate solutions, ignoring user input, or sounding like a generic helpful assistant "I can do that for you!") are checked by the carefully constructed prompt that keeps the AI in the **Consultant** or **Analyst** persona at all times.

**Blend of Clinical and AI Patterns for LLM_Gastro:** Ultimately, the research suggests LLM_Gastro's execution protocol should integrate these insights: Start every session with an _Engage_ phase that establishes trust and context (using greetings and empathy similar to a doctor's opening), then an _Analyze_ phase where the AI presents findings with appropriate hedging (like a doctor explaining test results with uncertainty language), a _Probe_ phase of iterative Q&A (enforcing minimum one question-answer turn, guided by evidence-based question frameworks - e.g., covering diet, stool form, timeline, psychosocial factors as in GI clinical practice), and only then a _Coach_ phase if needed (providing recommendations or action plans, and only if triggered by user interest or clear need). Each phase transition should have either an explicit user approval (as in Engage→Analyze→Probe gating) or an internal logic check (as in Analyze→Coach only if user asks or if a critical threshold of info is reached). The **tone throughout must remain patient-centered** - meaning the AI should frequently acknowledge the user's inputs and feelings ("I understand this is a lot of information..."), much as a well-trained physician would.

By combining the **content discipline of the VPA agent** (which brings rigorous conditional logic, formatted outputs, and refusal to act without criteria) with the **communication finesse of the Consultant agent** (which brings empathy, inquiry, and user partnership), LLM_Gastro can enforce its clinical protocol reliably. For example, if a user provides a symptom diary update (a dynamic JSON entry), LLM_Gastro might respond with a brief analysis of that entry (like a mini "CAS" - context analysis - checking if the new data fits prior patterns) and then say, _"I've updated your tracker. Before I give any thoughts, is there anything new you want to add or any specific concern today?"_, mimicking a doctor who would not only record data but also ask if the patient has new complaints. If the user then asks a question or indicates a problem, the agent transitions into the full consult mode (Probe further, then Coach). This dynamic adjustment is exactly what the **protocol triggering logic** aims for, and our analysis of VPA shows it's feasible to implement via prompt instructions.

Finally, both domains underscore a core principle: **never lose the human in the loop**. The physician-patient relationship thrives on listening, understanding, and guiding, and the AI-user interaction should too. By following the patterns identified - from motivational interviewing techniques to strict phase-based prompts - LLM_Gastro's agent can emulate a gastroenterology consultant that is **methodical yet compassionate**, providing accurate tracking and analysis while engaging the user in a human-like, supportive manner. As one guideline succinctly puts it, _"Effective communication creates a collaborative relationship, empowers the patient, and saves time by capturing relevant information"_. The prompt patterns we've examined are all geared towards that same goal: a collaborative AI that enforces doing the right thing (protocol adherence, no shortcuts) while communicating in a patient-friendly way. By merging these best practices, LLM_Gastro's execution protocol will be both **clinically validated** and **AI-aligned**, steering the agent to consistently behave like a diligent, empathetic gastroenterology consultant who always _asks before it tells_, and always keeps the user's needs and safety at the forefront.
