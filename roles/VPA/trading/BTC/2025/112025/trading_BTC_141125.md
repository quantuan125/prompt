## MACRO CONTEXT
### LAST WEEK

*(Context mainly for the week of Mon 3–Sun 9 Nov 2025, plus structural background that still drives markets now.)*

* **Tariff shock backdrop (“Liberation Day” tariffs):** Since 2 April 2025, Trump’s “Liberation Day” package has imposed a **baseline 10% tariff on almost all U.S. imports**, with much higher “reciprocal” rates on key partners (China, EU, UK etc.), producing the highest average U.S. tariff level in over a century and triggering a sharp global equity selloff back in April.  Markets are still trading against this regime as the macro baseline.

* **Tariffs vs China – temporary easing but still elevated:** Earlier in the year, the U.S. and China agreed to **ratchet down some of their April tariff hikes from ~125% back to 10%**, a reduction that has been repeatedly extended through **10 Nov 2025**, even as other “reciprocal” tariffs remain in place.  This leaves trade tensions structurally high, but with some relief on the most extreme China rates.

* **Fed navigating tariff-driven inflation risk:** Fed Vice Chair Jefferson has repeatedly stressed that **new tariffs are a key upside risk to inflation** and a reason to be cautious about cutting rates too quickly, even after the Fed’s latest **25 bp cut that took the policy range to about 4–4.25%**. ([Reuters][1]) His baseline view going into this week: growth slowing toward ~1.5% in 2025, labor market softening, and tariffs making the inflation outlook more uncertain.

* **Government shutdown drag and sentiment damage:** Up through last weekend, the U.S. was still in the midst of a **record-long government shutdown**, with consumer sentiment already down to a **3½-year low** and recent jobs data showing October payroll losses and rising unemployment worries. ([cfodive.com][2]) The shutdown delayed or disrupted major releases (CPI, PPI, jobs, retail sales), creating a data “fog” for both the Fed and markets.

* **Equities & crypto under tariff/shutdown overhang:** Equity indices had largely recovered from the April tariff crash but remained **very sensitive to tariff headlines and Fed cut odds**, with AI/mega-cap tech leading both rallies and selloffs. ([Business Standard][3]) Crypto traded as high-beta risk: earlier in November, Bitcoin briefly pushed back above six figures while still reacting to shutdown and rate-cut expectations.

In parallel, the administration is **preparing broad exemptions on some food-related tariffs** and has unveiled **trade frameworks with Argentina, Guatemala, Ecuador, and El Salvador** that lower duties on items like coffee, bananas and some beef to tackle voter anger over grocery prices.  The core “Liberation Day” tariff structure still stands; this is a targeted affordability tweak, not a wholesale reversal.

Markets now see December as a live but not guaranteed cut, making next week’s **FOMC minutes (Wed 19 Nov)** a key volatility event for rates, gold and risk assets.
